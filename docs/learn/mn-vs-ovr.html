Two ways to sort players into 9 types

Both get you the same result — every player labeled. They just take different routes. This shows where the two routes are identical, where they differ, and which to pick for our project. If you haven't read the two walkthroughs yet, start there.

Binary (yes/no) Multinomial (9-way) You are here: MN vs OvR

The two ideas, in one picture each

"Multinomial" (MN) and "One-vs-Rest" (OvR) are just two strategies for answering a 9-way question.

Strategy 1

Multinomial — one big 9-way sorter

One model considers all 9 types at once and outputs a probability for each — they add up to 100%. The biggest one wins.

Strategy 2

One-vs-Rest — nine yes/no gates

Build 9 separate yes/no models — one per type. Each says how sure it is; the most confident "yes" wins.

Walking the same steps — same or different?

Most steps are identical. The differences cluster around binning, baselines, and the final readout — and they're exactly the steps a scorecard modeler cares about.

1 · The goal differs
MultinomialOne question with 9 possible answers. 1 model.
One-vs-RestNine yes/no questions ("is it type X?"). 9 models.
2–4 · Meet data · explore · clean same
MultinomialIdentical — same table, same exploring, same fixing of blanks.
One-vs-RestIdentical — plus you can rank clues with Information Value, because each yes/no target is binary.
5 · Prep the clues differs
MultinomialRescale clues. WoE binning doesn't apply — it needs a two-group target, and MN has nine.
One-vs-RestYou get the full WoE / fine-coarse binning per model. This is the big one for scorecard fans.
6 · Drop redundant clues same
MultinomialVIF / varclus — identical.
One-vs-RestVIF / varclus — identical. (These look only at the clues, not the answer.)
7 · The baseline / target setup differs
MultinomialYou must pick a baseline type; every weight is "compared to that baseline." Extra step.
One-vs-RestNo baseline needed — "no" is automatically the comparison. Simpler here.
8 · Split & train same
MultinomialOne train/test split, one fit. Variable selection happens once, across all 9 types at once — a clue is in or out for everyone.
One-vs-RestSame split, but you fit (and do stepwise) 9 times independently — keep a clue for "grinder" even if you drop it for "new."
9 · Read what it learned differs
MultinomialEach clue has 8 weights (one per type vs the baseline). More to read; reference-relative.
One-vs-RestEach model has one clean weight per clue — the simplest possible readout. Nine tidy scorecards.
10 · Points scorecard differs
MultinomialNot available — there's no single score to scale into points (9 probabilities, not one).
One-vs-RestA real points scorecard per type — the classic FICO/SAS deliverable.
11 · Measure quality mostly same
MultinomialAccuracy + confusion matrix across 9 types.
One-vs-RestSame, plus KS / AUC per model — separation metrics you only get with a yes/no target.
12 · Set the decision differs
MultinomialArgmax — whichever type has the highest probability wins. No per-type knob.
One-vs-RestA separate cutoff per type — tune each one to what its mistakes cost.

So which should we use?

They're almost the same model under the hood. The real question is whether you want the scorecard control surface.

Pick Multinomial if…

You want one compact model, the simplest pipeline, and you're happy tuning with regularization + a baseline choice. Fewer moving parts, faster to ship. Good when nobody needs WoE/points/KS.

Pick One-vs-Rest if…

You want the full scorecard craft — WoE binning, Information Value, stepwise per model, points tables, KS — and a clean, defensible model per type. More models to maintain (nine), but every lever you're used to is there.

Our recommendation: One-vs-Rest. Our team has deep scorecard experience, and interpretability is the whole point of this layer. OvR is the only path that keeps every lever — binning, IV, stepwise, points, KS — and the "9 separate scorecards" shape is the honest shape of a 9-type problem anyway. The only cost is maintaining nine simple models instead of one slightly-less-tweakable one. The Python tools (toad, OptBinning, scorecardpy) are built for exactly this loop.
