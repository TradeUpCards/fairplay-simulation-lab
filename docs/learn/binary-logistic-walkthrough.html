How a yes/no model works — binary logistic

This is the simplest kind of model: it answers one yes/no question, like "Is this player a grinder?" It's also the building block of the one-vs-rest approach — run this nine times (one per type) and you've classified everyone. And it's where the classic credit-scorecard tools live.

You are here: Binary (yes/no) Multinomial (9-way) MN vs One-vs-Rest

The 30-second version

A binary logistic regression answers one yes/no question by weighing clues and outputting a probability ("82% chance this is a grinder"). You then pick a cutoff to turn that into a yes or no. It's the same machinery as the multinomial model but with only two answers — which makes it simpler and unlocks the scorecard toolkit (binning, Information Value, points, KS). Watch for the amber "scorecard" notes — those are the power-user steps a credit modeler would recognize.

1

The goal: answer one yes/no question

step 1 of 12
What this does

We pick one type and ask a single yes/no question about every player: "Is this player a grinder — yes or no?" Everyone who isn't a grinder is simply "no." Two possible answers, that's it.

Why it matters

Yes/no is the simplest possible model, so it's the best place to learn the ideas. It's also the building block of one-vs-rest: ask this 9 times (grinder? new? aggressive? …) and every player gets sorted.

Binary = two outcomes (yes/no). Logistic regression = a method that outputs the probability of "yes," which you then round to a decision.
2

Meet the data

step 2 of 12
What this does

Same table of players and clues as before, but the answer column is collapsed to YES (is a grinder) or NO (everything else).

Why it matters

This two-value answer is what unlocks the simplest math and the whole scorecard toolkit in the steps ahead.

Target = the yes/no column we predict. In scorecard language the two groups are often called "good" and "bad" — here, "grinder" vs "not."
3

Explore — and rank — the clues

step 3 of 12
What this does

We look at each clue's spread and — because there are only two groups — we can measure how well each clue separates the yes group from the no group, and rank them.

Why it matters

Up front you can see which clues actually carry signal (long, active players → grinder) and which are dead weight, before spending effort on them.

Scorecard tool

Information Value (IV) is the classic number for this — it scores each clue's power to separate good from bad. Rough rule of thumb: under 0.02 = useless, 0.1–0.3 = decent, over 0.5 = strong. This only exists because the target is binary.

4

Fix missing & odd values

step 4 of 12
What this does

Blanks get filled with a sensible value, and impossible values get corrected — same as any model.

Why it matters

The math can't run on blanks, and one bad value skews everything.

Scorecard tool

In scorecards you often don't just fill a blank — you give "missing" its own bin. Sometimes the fact that a value is missing is itself a useful clue, and binning lets you keep that signal instead of hiding it.

5

Scale the clues — or bin them with WoE

step 5 of 12
What this does

The plain path: rescale clues like before. The scorecard path: chop each clue into bins (e.g. hand-count ranges) and replace each bin with a number that says how strongly that range points to "yes."

Why it matters

Binning tames outliers, handles bends in the relationship, and makes the final model read like a human-friendly table of ranges instead of raw math.

Scorecard tool

Weight of Evidence (WoE) is the number each bin gets — positive = "this range leans yes," negative = "leans no." You usually tidy bins so the trend only goes one direction (monotonic): more hands → more grinder-ish, never zig-zagging. "Coarse vs fine binning" is just how many bins you cut.

6

Drop redundant clues

step 6 of 12
What this does

Just like in any model: if two clues overlap heavily, keep one. Correlation, VIF, and varclus find the twins.

Why it matters

Overlapping clues split the credit between them, which can flip a clue's sign and make the model hard to explain — a cardinal sin in a scorecard you have to defend.

VIF / varclus = tools that flag overlapping clues so you can keep one representative from each group. Identical to the multinomial step — this one transfers over unchanged.
7

Split: study set vs quiz set

step 7 of 12
What this does

Teach on ~80% of players, hide ~20% for an honest final grade. Same golden rule as the multinomial model.

Why it matters

A model that's only graded on data it studied will look great and fail in real life. The hidden set keeps you honest.

Scorecard tool

Credit modelers often add a third slice: an out-of-time sample (players from a later period) to check the model still holds up as the world changes. Optional, but it's why scorecards age gracefully.

8

Train — and add/remove clues

step 8 of 12
What this does

We fit the model on the study set, then add and remove clues one at a time, keeping only the ones that genuinely improve it. The model learns one weight per clue (just yes-vs-no — no per-type weights to juggle).

Why it matters

A lean model with only clues that pull their weight is more accurate on new players and far easier to explain and defend.

Scorecard tool

Stepwise selection is the formal version of add/remove, using statistical significance (p-values) to decide. Because there's only one yes/no target, you get one clean p-value per clue — exactly the control surface a SAS/FICO modeler is used to.

9

Read what it learned

step 9 of 12
What this does

Each clue gets one weight: positive pushes toward "yes," negative toward "no." High lifetime_hands and long avg_session push toward grinder; high vpip pushes away.

Why it matters

Because there are only two answers, the readout is even cleaner than the multinomial model — one number per clue, no baseline to keep straight. Easy reason codes.

Think of it like a points checklist: each clue adds or subtracts from a running "grinder score," and the total decides the call.
10

Turn it into a points scorecard

step 10 of 12
What this does

The weights can be rescaled into round points so the model becomes a literal scorecard: this range = +40, that range = −15, add them up, and a higher total means a stronger "yes."

Why it matters

A points table is something anyone — even non-technical reviewers — can read, audit, and trust. It's why credit scores look the way they do.

Scorecard tool

This is the "points to double the odds" scaling from FICO/SAS scorecards. It only exists for a yes/no model — a 9-way model has no single score to scale, which is one reason a scorecard modeler often prefers one-vs-rest.

11

How good is it? (separation & KS)

step 11 of 12
What this does

We score the hidden quiz players and check whether grinders pile up at high scores and non-grinders at low scores. The more the two groups separate, the better the model.

Why it matters

For a yes/no model, separation matters more than raw accuracy — especially when one answer is rarer than the other.

Scorecard tool

The KS statistic measures exactly this — the biggest gap between the two groups' score curves. Higher KS = cleaner separation. AUC / ROC is the close cousin. These are the headline metrics on every credit-scorecard report.

12

Pick the cutoff (yes vs no)

step 12 of 12
What this does

The model outputs a probability; you pick a cutoff (say 50%) above which you call it "yes." A 2×2 confusion table shows the trade-off: move the line and you catch more grinders but also mislabel more non-grinders.

Why it matters

The right cutoff depends on what a mistake costs. In a yes/no model you control this directly — a flexibility you lose in the all-at-once multinomial model.

You're done. You have a yes/no model, optionally a points scorecard, separation/KS to prove it works, and a cutoff you control. Do this nine times — once per type — and you've built one-vs-rest.  See Multinomial vs One-vs-Rest →
