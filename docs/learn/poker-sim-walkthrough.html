How the play simulation works — agents that actually play hands

Instead of hand-setting each player's stats, we let an agent play real hands in the archetype's style. Realistic table dynamics fall out — and the signals our detector watches (looseness, aggression, soft-play, robotic timing) emerge from the play rather than being typed in.

You are here: Play simulation Archetype profiles (spec) The detector (binary logistic)

The 30-second version

Build one decision engine and give it ten knob-sets — one per archetype. The engine reads the game state (cards, position, stacks, pot, opponents), estimates equity, and the knobs bias every choice: how loose, how aggressive, how big, how human. Run thousands of seeded hands and each agent's realized stats (vpip, pfr, aggression factor) settle onto the archetype's real targets. The integrity behaviours — collusion soft-play, robotic timing, promo-grinding — are extra policy layers. The point: the detector's features become emergent properties of play, so the simulation is a genuine test of the detector, not a field it reads back.

1

Why simulate actual play?

step 1 of 9
What this does

Today P2's generator writes each player's stats directly. Here we instead let an agent play hands in character; the stats are then measured from how it played.

Why it matters

It produces real table dynamics — a predator actually attacks the new player, a colluder actually soft-plays a teammate — and the detector's signals emerge from that. That makes the simulation a true test: the detector can't just read a field it was handed.

The guiding principle. Design the engine so vpip, aggression, soft_play_delta, timing_regularity and co-seating are emergent properties of play — not inputs. If they're typed in, the detector is grading its own homework.
Responsible useSandbox only. These agents play synthetic chips in the integrity lab so the detector can be tested. This is not a tool for real-money play, real-time assistance, or actual collusion (see CLAUDE.md non-goals).
2

One engine, ten personalities

step 2 of 9
What this does

We don't write ten programs. We write one decision engine and hand it a vector of knobs for each archetype: looseness, preflop aggression, postflop aggression, sizing, skill, stamina, risk-aversion, plus integrity flags.

Why it matters

It keeps the agents comparable and the differences auditable — a grinder and a recreational differ only by their knobs, and you can read exactly which knob makes them play differently.

Knob vector = the small set of numbers that turns the generic engine into one archetype. The whole spec lives in docs/archetype-play-profiles.md.
3

A stat is just a knob

step 3 of 9
What this does

Each aggregate stat maps to a decision dial. vpip sets how wide a range the agent enters; pfr sets how often it raises instead of limping; the aggression factor sets how often it bets/raises vs calls after the flop.

Why it matters

These are the targets the agent must hit. A grinder's dials (vpip 0.23, pfr 0.21, AF 2.69) say "tight, almost never limps, aggressive postflop." A recreational's (0.38 / 0.12 / 1.18) say "loose and passive."

statdial
vpippreflop looseness (entering-range width)
pfrpreflop aggression; vpip − pfr = the limp/call gap
aggression_factorpostflop aggression (bet/raise vs call)
avg_pot_sizebet sizing / stakes
4

What the engine looks at

step 4 of 9
What this does

Before choosing an action the engine reads the full situation, not just its two cards: position, effective stack and stack-to-pot ratio, the board texture, the pot and the price to call, how many players are in, and the betting action so far.

Why it matters

Position and stack depth swing the right play more than the cards do. A real-feeling agent has to weigh them — and weighing them badly is exactly how a low-skill archetype (new, recreational) leaks chips.

SPR = stack ÷ pot. Low SPR → commit with top pair; high SPR → play more carefully and chase implied odds. Position = how late you act; acting last is a big edge.
Like chess: the same piece is strong or weak depending on the whole board. The engine scores the situation, then the knobs decide how boldly to act on it.
5

Playing one hand, street by street

step 5 of 9
What this does

On each street the engine: (1) estimates equity — its chance to win against the opponents' likely range; (2) lets the knobs bias the threshold — looseness lowers the equity needed to continue, aggression turns a marginal call into a bet; (3) picks an action and a size.

Why it matters

This loop is the heart of "playing." Same engine, different knobs → a grinder value-bets a thin edge while a recreational just calls and a predator turns it into a bluff-raise.

Scorecard parallelUse mixed strategies — sometimes call, sometimes raise the same hand, chosen by a seeded coin-flip — so play isn't robotic. The one exception is the bot, whose lack of mixing is its tell (step 8).
Equity = win probability vs a range. Range = the set of hands an opponent could hold, narrowed as they act. EV = expected chips; the engine prefers higher-EV actions, knob-biased.
6

Reading — and exploiting — opponents

step 6 of 9
What this does

Each agent keeps reads on the others (their observed looseness/aggression) and adjusts: value-bet thinner against a calling-station, bluff more against a nit, tighten up against a maniac. A predator carries a "target the weakest player" flag and hunts the new/recreational seat.

Why it matters

This is where the table dynamics emerge. Predation pressure isn't a number we set — it's what happens when a predator agent actually attacks the fish. The healthy_anchor deliberately does not hunt; that's why it stabilises a table.

Emergence again. "This table is dangerous for a beginner" becomes true in the simulation — the new player really does bust faster — which is exactly the table-health signal the detector later has to catch.
7

Being human: tilt, fatigue, timing

step 7 of 9
What this does

Layer on human realism: tilt (play looser/wilder after a bad beat, or quit), fatigue (decisions decay over a long session), a stop-loss / win-goal (when to leave), and a decision-latency model (how long it "thinks" before acting).

Why it matters

These produce the session and timing signals. A new player's low stop-loss is the 12-minute churn in CASE-A. And the latency model is where timing_regularity comes from — humans vary, so a near-flat latency curve is the bot's fingerprint.

Action timing = the seconds before each decision. Modelling it explicitly (with per-archetype jitter) means the bot's robotic consistency emerges as a detectable signal.
8

Coordinated & special behaviour

step 8 of 9
What this does

Integrity archetypes get extra policy layers. A cluster agent is member-aware: it soft-plays teammates (won't bet into them, folds marginal spots, can chip-dump) and gangs up on outsiders, while members converge on the same tables.

Why it matters

The collusion signals — negative soft_play_delta, high co-seating, outsider pressure — are produced by this behaviour, not set by hand. That's what makes CASE-C a real positive the detector must find.

Don't over-fit the trap. The shared_device_household agents share a device but are two independent players — divergent schedules, no soft-play, rarely the same table. They must look superficially linked yet behave independently, so the detector learns not to escalate them (CASE-E). The bot is its own layer: deterministic policy, flat timing, no tilt, near-24/7.
9

Closing the loop: calibrate & feed the detector

step 9 of 9
What this does

Run each agent over thousands of seeded hands, measure its realized vpip/pfr/AF, and tune the knobs until they hit the archetype's targets from the profile spec. Then the hand histories aggregate into the same player features our pipeline already uses.

Why it matters

This is the bridge between agents playing hands and the fields the scoring engine consumes — and seeding keeps every run reproducible, matching the lab's deterministic ethos. The simulation slots in under the current generator with Contract-1 compatibility.

You've closed the loop. Agents play → signals emerge → calibration locks them to the real archetype targets → the hand histories feed Contract 1 → the detector scores them. Because the signals are emergent, beating the detector now means actually changing how the agents play — a genuine test.  See the archetype spec →
