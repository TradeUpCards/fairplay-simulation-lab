How a multinomial model sorts players into types

We have thousands of poker players and want a program to label each one as one of 9 "types." This walks through exactly how that program is built — one plain-English step at a time, with a picture for each.

You are here: Multinomial Binary logistic (yes/no) MN vs One-vs-Rest

The 30-second version

A multinomial logistic regression is a recipe that learns, from examples, how to guess which of several types something is. Here it looks at a player's habits (how often they bet, how long they play, etc.) and outputs the chance they're each of the 9 types — then picks the most likely one. "Multinomial" just means more than two possible answers. The 12 steps below are how you build and check one.

1

The goal: sort each player into a type

step 1 of 12
What this does

For every player, the program reads their stats and decides which of 9 types (archetypes) they are — like new, recreational, grinder, or aggressive. That label then drives everything else the system does.

Why it matters

Downstream features (table health, seating recommendations) all need to know each player's type. Labeling thousands of players by hand is impossible; a model does it the same way every time.

Think of it like a school guidance counselor who, after seeing enough students, can glance at someone's habits and say "you're a band kid" or "you're a math-team kid." The model is a counselor that learned the patterns from examples.
Multinomial logistic regression = a method that, given some clues, outputs the probability of each possible answer (here, 9 of them) and picks the highest. "Multinomial" = many possible answers.
2

Meet the data

step 2 of 12
What this does

We lay the data out as a table. Each row is one player (122 of them). Each column is one clue about that player — there are 16, like vpip (how often they play a hand), pfr, lifetime_hands, and promo_redemptions.

Why it matters

You can't sort what you don't understand. Before any math, you want to know exactly what one row means and what each column is measuring.

Feature = a clue/column the model gets to look at. Target = the answer we want it to predict (the player's type). Features are the inputs; the target is the output.
3

Explore the clues

step 3 of 12
What this does

We look at the spread of each clue. Is vpip usually between 0.2 and 0.6? Are there blanks? Any impossible values (a vpip of 5, when it should be 0–1)? A simple bar chart per clue shows this fast.

Why it matters

Surprises here — a missing clue, a typo, a freak value — will quietly mislead the model later. It's far cheaper to catch them now than to debug a weird result at the end.

Think of it like reading the ingredients before you cook. If the milk smells off, you find out now, not after it's baked into the cake.
4

Fix missing & odd values

step 4 of 12
What this does

Wherever a clue is blank, we fill it in with a reasonable stand-in (often the typical value for that clue), or flag it. Impossible values get corrected or capped.

Why it matters

The math literally can't run on a blank cell, and a single crazy value can drag the whole result off course. Clean inputs are the price of trustworthy outputs.

Imputation = the fancy word for "filling in a missing value with a sensible guess" so the row is still usable.
5

Put the clues on the same scale

step 5 of 12
What this does

Some clues are huge numbers (lifetime_hands can be 285,000) and some are tiny (vpip is around 0.3). We rescale them so they all sit in a comparable range.

Why it matters

Without this, the model assumes the big-number clue is more important just because it's bigger — which isn't true. Rescaling lets every clue compete fairly.

Standardizing = rescaling each clue so it's measured in "how far from average," not raw units. After it, "tall for a player" and "rich for a player" are on the same yardstick.
6

Drop redundant clues

step 6 of 12
What this does

If two clues basically measure the same thing (say, two different aggression stats), we keep one and drop the other. Tools like correlation, VIF, or varclus spot these twins.

Why it matters

Redundant clues confuse the model (it can't tell which twin deserves the credit) and muddy the "why." Fewer, distinct clues = a simpler, steadier, more explainable model.

Multicollinearity = the long word for "two or more clues overlap too much." VIF and varclus are just tools that measure and flag the overlap.
7

Pick a baseline type (this step is special to multinomial)

step 7 of 12
What this does

A multinomial model describes every type relative to one chosen "default" type. We pick a sensible baseline — say recreational — and then every result reads as "compared to a recreational player, this clue makes them more/less likely to be type X."

Why it matters

The model needs an anchor to compare against. A well-chosen baseline (a common, "ordinary" type) makes all the results easy to read.

This is the one step you won't see in a yes/no model. A binary model only has two answers, so it doesn't need to pick a baseline — the "no" side is the baseline. See the binary walkthrough.
8

Split: study set vs quiz set

step 8 of 12
What this does

We teach the model on about 80% of the players (the "study set") and quietly hide the other 20% (the "quiz set") until the very end.

Why it matters

We want proof the model works on players it has never seen — not that it just memorized the answers. The hidden quiz set is the honest test.

Training data = the study set the model learns from. Test data = the hidden quiz set we grade it on. Keeping them separate is the golden rule of honest modeling.
9

Train the model

step 9 of 12
What this does

We run the algorithm on the study set. It searches for the weight of each clue (for each type) that best separates the 9 types — essentially turning dials until its guesses line up with the real answers.

Why it matters

This is the actual learning — turning a pile of examples into a reusable rule the model can apply to brand-new players.

Weight (or coefficient) = how strongly a clue pushes toward a type. Big positive weight = "this clue is strong evidence for this type." This is what the model is solving for.
10

Read what it learned (the reason codes)

step 10 of 12
What this does

Each clue ends up with a weight per type. We read them off: a big positive weight means "this clue is strong evidence for this type." For example, high pfr and vpip push toward aggressive; a long average session pushes away from it.

Why it matters

This is the big payoff of this kind of model — its reasoning is readable. These weights become the "reason codes" you can show a human: "flagged as aggressive because of high pre-flop raise rate."

Think of it like a tug-of-war: each clue pulls the player toward or away from each type, and the strongest pull wins.
11

How often is it right?

step 11 of 12
What this does

We unhide the quiz set, let the model guess each player's type, and compare to the real answers. The simplest score is accuracy — the share it got right.

Why it matters

This is the honest report card, on players the model never studied. A high score on the study set means nothing; a high score here is what counts.

Accuracy = (number it got right) ÷ (total players in the quiz set). One number, easy to explain — though Step 12 shows why it's not the whole story.
12

Where does it get confused?

step 12 of 12
What this does

A confusion matrix is a grid: rows are the true type, columns are the guess. The green diagonal is "got it right." Off-diagonal amber cells show which types it mixes up — e.g. it sometimes calls a regular a recreational.

Why it matters

One accuracy number hides patterns. Knowing where it fails tells you what to fix — and a final sanity check ("do the weights make sense?") tells you whether to trust it at all.

You're done. You now have a model that labels any player, an honest score, a map of its weak spots, and readable reasons for each call. That's a finished, defensible multinomial classifier.  Next: see how this compares to the one-vs-rest approach →
