Finding the groups: do our archetypes really exist?

We have hunches about player types — but what if the data disagrees? Clustering lets the data vote on its own groupings, with no answers given. This is how you check (or discover) a taxonomy before you commit to it.

You are here: Clustering Multinomial Binary / OvR MN vs OvR

★ Why we do this — and the one big caveat

Classification (the other guides) assigns players to types you've already decided on. Clustering does the opposite: it has no labels and finds natural groups by similarity. You'd use it when you think you know the segments but want the data to confirm or refine them — exactly our situation.

But here's the honest caveat for our project: our player data is synthetic — it was generated from the archetype rules. So clustering it will mostly rediscover the rules we put in. That's not a failure; it just changes what clustering is for here:

On our synthetic data (now)

Clustering mostly confirms the workflow — it'll recover our 9 groups because we built them in. Value: it demonstrates the method on data where we know the right answer (like the teaching notebooks recovering known rules). Not required for the demo; classification is the deliverable.

On real player data (later)

Clustering is essential. With real players you genuinely don't know the segments — the data might merge two of your guesses, split one in three, or surface a type you never imagined. Here clustering earns its keep as real discovery, before any classifier is trained.

So: read this as the method we'll lean on hard with real data, demonstrated now on synthetic data where we can check our work. The 10 steps below are the same in both worlds — only the stakes change.
1

The question: what groups are even in here?

step 1 of 10
What this does

We start with players placed by their stats but no type labels at all. The question isn't "is this a grinder?" — it's "are there natural clumps in here, and how many?"

Why it matters

This is the only honest way to ask whether your assumed archetypes are real. If you only ever classify into pre-decided types, you can never discover you were wrong about them.

Unsupervised = learning with no answer key. The model groups by similarity alone. (Classification is supervised — it's given the answers.)
2

Meet the data — note the missing column

step 2 of 10
What this does

Same clue columns as the classifier (vpip, hands, sessions…), but we ignore the type column entirely — clustering never sees it.

Why it matters

The whole point is to see what the clues alone suggest. (On synthetic data we secretly know the answer — we'll use it in Step 8 only to grade the clusters, never to build them.)

3

Put the clues on the same scale

step 3 of 10
What this does

Rescale every clue to a comparable range — same as in the classifier, but here it's even more critical.

Why it matters

Clustering groups by distance. If lifetime_hands is in the hundred-thousands and vpip is around 0.3, distance is dominated entirely by hands and every other clue is ignored. Scaling makes "close" mean genuinely similar.

Distance = how far apart two players sit across all clues. Clustering puts players who are "close" in the same group — so the yardstick has to be fair.
4

How many groups? (the big question)

step 4 of 10
What this does

We try several values for the number of groups (K = 2, 3, 4… up to ~15) and measure how tidy the groups are at each. We look for the "elbow" — where adding more groups stops helping much.

Why it matters

This directly answers "is it really 9 archetypes?" Maybe the data clearly prefers 6, or 11. You let the evidence pick the number instead of assuming it.

Elbow plot and silhouette score are two ways to judge "what's a good K." For GMM (next steps) there's an even cleaner score called BIC.
5

K-means — sort into hard groups

step 5 of 10
What this does

K-means drops K "centers" into the data and assigns each player to the nearest one, then nudges the centers and repeats until they settle. Every player ends up in exactly one group.

Why it matters

It's the fast, robust first read — especially good on a small dataset like ours. It gives you concrete groups to inspect right away.

Think of it like three teachers standing in a field; every student walks to the nearest teacher, the teachers shuffle to the middle of their crowd, and everyone re-picks — until nobody moves.
6

GMM — allow soft, fuzzy membership

step 6 of 10
What this does

A Gaussian Mixture Model is the "soft" cousin of K-means: instead of forcing each player into one group, it gives a probability of each group — "60% grinder, 40% regular." It also allows stretched, different-sized groups.

Why it matters

Players are genuinely in-between sometimes; soft membership captures that honestly. GMM's BIC score is also a principled way to pick the number of groups. (Trade-off: it wants more data than K-means, so on our small set treat it as a second opinion.)

GMM = soft clustering with probabilities + flexible shapes. BIC = a score that rewards fit but penalizes too many groups — pick the K with the best BIC.
7

Name the clusters

step 7 of 10
What this does

Each cluster is just "Cluster 2" until we look inside it. We read its typical stats (high hands, long sessions, low vpip → patient and serious) and give it a human name like grinder.

Why it matters

Raw cluster numbers mean nothing to a business. Naming turns a math output into a real archetype people can reason about and act on.

8

Compare to our hunches

step 8 of 10
What this does

Now we lay the discovered clusters next to our assumed archetypes. Do they line up? Did the data merge two of our guesses into one, split one into two, or surface something we didn't expect?

Why it matters

This is the entire payoff. On real data, a split or merge here means your taxonomy was wrong — and you just found out before betting the whole system on it. (On our synthetic data, expect a clean match — that's the workflow confirming itself.)

9

Lock the taxonomy

step 9 of 10
What this does

We settle on the final archetype list — keeping, merging, or splitting based on what the clusters showed — and freeze it.

Why it matters

Everything downstream (the classifier, the scoring, the UI) hard-codes these names. You want them evidence-backed and stable before that, not changing under you mid-build.

10

Hand off to the classifier

step 10 of 10
What this does

The named clusters become the labels we train a supervised classifier on. Clustering discovered and validated the types; the classifier (multinomial or one-vs-rest) then assigns every future player to one.

Why it matters

This is the full, honest pipeline: discover the groups → name them → classify into them. You never assumed the answer — you earned it, then operationalized it.

You're done. You now know whether your archetypes are real, what they are, and you've handed clean labels to the classifier.  Next: the multinomial or one-vs-rest classifier turns those labels into a model.
