FairPlay IQ · Decision v2 (evolved from v1) · Built to defend on two fronts

We are using AI agents for gameplay. The decision is how.

The team committed to AI agents in gameplay — so this guide is about doing it correctly, not arguing it away. It shows the evolution from v1 ("the LLM never plays") to v2 ("agents play — but tool-grounded, scoped, and frozen"), with the evidence, so you can defend the call against both the "no LLM in gameplay" skeptic and the "make agents do everything" maximalist.

You are here: the v2 decision & how it evolved Decision doc
The decision in one sentence

Build one agent-based simulator (PokerKit environment, an agent per seat). Choose each agent's brain — seeded policy vs. live LLM — per seat, by whether reasoning is the product there. Run two loops: a deterministic health/routing backtest (the headline) and a scoped LLM collusion case (integrity). Freeze every run so the demo is reproducible — and never generate health outcomes from the health model itself.

AEvolution: v1 → v2 (a history, not a retcon)

The v1 reasoning still holds. What changed is the driver of the integrity-critical actions — and four things made that flip defensible rather than a cave.

BThe decision: an agent-based simulator, and each agent's brain

"Agent" does not mean "LLM." A table is an environment, each seat is an agent, the room is the simulation — Pluribus, RLCard, OpenSpiel are all "agents," none are LLMs. The architecture is fixed. The only choice is each agent's brain (game-state → action), and there are four — all still agents in the same simulator.

The criterion: give a seat an LLM + tools brain only when the behavior is what's being tested (the coordinated-pair collusion; optionally bot-like). Every other seat — and the entire 8-hour room — gets a seeded brain. Freeze and replay all of it; live agents sit behind a feature flag. This isn't "agents vs. policies" — it's one simulator where we pick a brain per seat.
Why the one LLM-brained case is worth it: it closes the gap our own simulator-signal-gap.md named ("derive, don't assert"). Today we assert soft_play_delta; with two tool-grounded agents secretly colluding, we derive it from real play — "two AI agents colluded; our system caught them," which a scripted policy cannot honestly claim.

CThe two loops — and why each wants a different brain

The simulator serves two validation loops. They're easy to conflate, and they want different brains. The first is the headline.

Headline · seeded brains · controlled

◆ Health / routing loop

  • Question: does the router's seating produce healthier tables?
  • Generate: the whole table, all archetypes, over 8 hours
  • Measure: realized Health(T) — winnings concentration, rec-loss velocity, retention
  • Validates: ΔHealth, the router, the whole table-health thesis
  • Brain: seeded policies — a controlled experiment needs reproducible inputs
TWO LOOPS
Integrity · LLM+tools · emergence

✦ Integrity loop

  • Question: does the scorer catch bad behavior?
  • Generate: the colluding pair
  • Measure: SOFT_PLAYS_AGAINST fires from real play
  • Validates: the evidence / integrity engine
  • Brain: LLM + tools — emergence of the collusion is the product

The health/routing loop is the closed-loop backtest of the product's core claim. The router predicts ΔHealth when it seats a player; the only way to know it was right is to play the table out and measure realized health, then compare. Today room_metrics_*.json assert the Standard-vs-FairPlay divergence; the simulator derives it — the demo's third link made real.

⚠ The circularity guardrail — makes or breaks the backtest. If the same model that scores health also generates the gameplay, the validation is a tautology — the router "succeeds" by construction. The generator's sense of who wins, loses, busts, and leaves must come from independent first-principles poker economics (actual chip flow, equity realized by the engine, EV by skill) — never from re-running Health(T)'s own formula. Then realized health, measured bottom-up, is a genuine independent check on the router's top-down prediction.

DThe five facts — now constraints, not vetoes

In v1 these argued against LLM gameplay. In v2 they don't forbid agents — they shape how we use them (tool-grounded not raw; frozen not live; persona = archetype). Most cleared 3-of-3 adversarial verification; a few (badged "verified vs. source") were checked directly against the primary source afterward. Click any fact to drill in.

1 The data has no archetype labels — and almost nothing FairPlay needs. verified 3-0 ×4 PHH format spec · arXiv 2312.11753v3 ▶

The PHH format's complete field inventory — required (variant, antes, blinds_or_straddles, starting_stacks, actions) and optional (players, seats, venue, time…) — contains no field for player style, skill, rating, or archetype (the players field is just names/mononyms). Worse for us, it also lacks account metadata, table-selection behavior, mocked devices, cluster/household relationships, long-session health metrics, and integrity labels — essentially the entire FairPlay substrate.

So the founding premise is false. "The AI learns the archetypes from the data" can't happen — there are no archetypes in the data, nor the device/relationship/health substrate we score on. Whether you fine-tune or prompt, you must construct it all yourself. That removes the main reason to reach for a learned model at all.
Source: arXiv 2312.11753v3 · github.com/uoftcprg/phh-std · full field list also at phh.readthedocs.io
1b And the corpus is the wrong shape — ~620M of its hands are heads-up bots. verified vs. source Zenodo API · record 17136841 ▶

The dataset is big — ~20.3 GB, ~641.7M hands — but the headline number is a trap. About 620M of those are ACPC computer-competition hands: 278.8M heads-up (2-player) no-limit + 341M heads-up/3-player fixed-limit, all bot-vs-bot. Only 21.6M are real-money, multi-handed online NLHE (HandHQ, 2009); add 10k Pluribus + 83 WSOP hands.

Heads-up bot hands are useless for FairPlay. We score 6-max table composition, collusion, and human account behavior — none of which exist in 2-player bot play. The usable slice is ~3% of the headline, and it's a realism reference for distributions, never a FairPlay simulator. (This is also why "but it's 600M+ hands!" is not the argument it sounds like.)
Source: zenodo.org/api/records/17136841 (verbatim composition + 20.3 GB file size)
2 Our own hard rules forbid LLM-generated hands in the simulator. project rule CLAUDE.md ▶

CLAUDE.md is explicit: "The simulator and scoring are seeded and reproducible" and "The LLM is never the detector." LLM hand generation is non-deterministic by nature, and at our stated scale of 50–150 hands per case it is slow and costly. A non-deterministic generator in the demo spine breaks the definition of done: "run the whole demo from static fixtures, then swap to computed data without changing the story."

Determinism is non-negotiable here. The only way any LLM-in-the-loop generation survives this rule is the "frozen JSON" escape hatch — generate once, commit the output, replay deterministically (Approach C, and only for a handful of showcase hands).
Source: project CLAUDE.md · echoed in DECISIONS.md D0/D1
3 A mature, MIT-licensed, deterministic engine already does the hard part. verified 3-0 ×6 PokerKit · arXiv 2308.07327 (IEEE ToG) ▶

PokerKit — from the same University of Toronto group that publishes PHH — is pure Python, MIT-licensed, supports No-Limit Texas Hold'em with full legal-move / game-state / showdown logic, evaluates >1,000,000 hands/second, and deals deterministically via explicit card control. The paper positions it expressly "for developing, testing, and benchmarking poker AI."

The gameplay substrate is solved and free. We don't build a poker engine or train a bot — we drive a battle-tested one. That's why the engine path is less effort than the LLM path, not more.
Source: arXiv 2308.07327 · github.com/uoftcprg/pokerkit (MIT)
4 Our archetype targets are already fields we own. in-repo data/players.json ▶

players.json already carries vpip, pfr, aggression_factor, avg_pot_size_bb, soft_play_delta, timing_regularity. A parametric archetype policy is simply: "play seeded hands so the realized statistics hit these target values, and for cluster members inject the seeded soft-play / chip-dump."

No model training is needed to express an archetype. The archetype is a set of target statistics we already defined. The engine just plays toward them.
Source: data/players.json · classifier thresholds in scoring/classify.py
5 Standalone LLMs are weak poker players — and the fix is exactly our approach. verified vs. source PokerBench 2501.08328 · ToolPoker 2602.00528 ▶

Two papers converge. PokerBench (AAAI 2025) shows LLMs need fine-tuning to approach optimal play and is GTO-anchored — not a style resource. ToolPoker (arXiv 2602.00528) finds LLMs fail against traditional algorithms, with three recurring flaws — heuristic play, factual mistakes, and a "knowing-doing gap" (actions diverge from their own reasoning) — and only reach strong play by bolting on external CFR/equity solvers. That is precisely our engine-plays / LLM-explains split.

The "use AI for styled hands" plan assumes a solved problem that isn't. Style-conditioned LLM poker has no robust prior art, and the literature's own answer to "LLMs are bad at poker" is add a solver — i.e. use the engine. Honest caveat: one new contrarian paper (PokerSkill, 2605.30094) claims expert play without solvers; we bet on the reproducible solver-backed path, not a single fresh result.
Sources: arXiv 2501.08328 (PokerBench) · arXiv 2602.00528 (ToolPoker) · cf. 2605.30094 (PokerSkill, contra)

FWhere the AI agents go now

v2 adds a fourth home — agents that genuinely play — but scoped and frozen. The other three are language-side and unchanged.

Home 0 · NEW in v2 · gameplay

Tool-grounded agents that play the collusion showcase

LLM agents whose persona is the archetype, reasoning in character but calling a CFR/equity solver for the action (ToolPoker pattern), refereed by PokerKit. Two agents share a side-channel → emergent collusion the scorer catches. Generated once, frozen, committed as fixtures.

Guardrails: tool-grounded (not raw) · offline + frozen (live only behind a feature flag) · one showcase, not every hand · still not the detector.
Home 1 · already the plan

The AI Investigator

Explains the structured evidence packet to a pit boss in plain language — why it's risky, why it might be innocent, what a human should do. This is the headline AI feature of the capstone.

Guardrail: reads only the structured packet, never raw rows; never the detector.
Home 2 · training sim (offline)

Coaching & table-talk

The bot's decisions come from the engine/solver (deterministic, fast, actually strong). The LLM sits on top for narration and "here's why that line was a mistake" coaching.

Guardrail: separate decision from explanation — and keep it offline / operator-facing. A player-facing coach that advises during live play is Real-Time Assistance (RTA), which poker treats as cheating.
Home 3 · evals

Adversarial scenario authoring

The LLM proposes specs for sneakier collusion or trickier false-positives; a human reviews them; the engine then plays them out deterministically. The LLM authors the test, not the hands.

Guardrail: human-in-the-loop; engine produces the actual play.

GDefend it — on two fronts

v2 gets pushback from two directions: the determinism skeptic ("don't put an LLM in gameplay") and the maximalist ("make agents do everything"). Rehearse both until you can give them cold.

Front 1 — the determinism skeptic

?"Putting an LLM in gameplay breaks our seeded/reproducible rule."▶
Your answer
It doesn't touch the reproducible path. The agents generate offline, once; we commit the output; the demo replays the frozen file. The seed is the file — the same "frozen JSON" pattern our DECISIONS.md D0-option-C already endorses. Live agents only run behind a feature flag for an optional "watch it generate" view, never in the canonical demo.
?"LLMs play bad poker — the hands won't be realistic."▶
Your answer
Raw ones do (ToolPoker, 2602.00528). Ours are tool-grounded — they reason in-persona but call a CFR/equity solver for the action, which is the research's own fix and how LLM agents reach pro-level play. PokerKit referees legality, and we validate the output falls in-distribution vs. the 21.6M real PHH hands. Realism is checked, not trusted.
?"Isn't the LLM now the detector — the thing the rules forbid?"▶
Your answer
No. The gameplay agent and the scorer are separate components. The agent creates behavior (offline, frozen); the structured scorer finds risk from the hand stream; the Investigator only explains. Nothing about agents playing moves the LLM into the detection path.
?"Then fine-tune a model on the 21.6 million PHH hands."▶
Your answer
Those hands have no archetype labels and are CC-BY 2009-era data. Fine-tuning still makes us synthesize the conditioning signal ourselves, it's hard to make deterministic and controllable, and it's the highest-effort, lowest-ROI option for a capstone (Approach D). We'd spend the whole sprint on a model that we then can't fully steer — versus parametric policies we can dial to any target stat today.

Common follow-ups (either front)

?"It's 600+ million hands — surely that's enough to learn realistic play from."▶
Your answer
It's a headline trap. ~620M of those ~641M hands are ACPC computer-competition hands — heads-up or 3-handed bots playing bots. FairPlay scores 6-max table composition, collusion, and human account behavior, none of which exist in 2-player bot logs. The genuinely relevant slice — real-money, multi-handed human NLHE — is the 21.6M HandHQ subset (~3%), and even that has no account metadata, devices, relationships, or integrity labels. Big number, wrong shape.
?"Let's make it a slick AI coach that helps players improve in real time."▶
Your answer
A real-time coach that advises a player during live play is Real-Time Assistance (RTA) — the poker world treats that as cheating, which is the exact behavior FairPlay exists to flag. An offline practice-vs-bots trainer is fine (that's what GTO Wizard's trainer is), but cleanest of all for an operator product is to frame any sim as operator training / demo, not a player decision coach. We don't want to ship the thing we detect.

Front 2 — the maximalist

?"Why not give every seat an LLM brain — agents for the whole simulation?"▶
Your answer
The whole sim is agents already — just with seeded brains. Giving them LLM brains breaks the headline: the health/routing loop is a controlled experiment (Standard vs. FairPlay, same seed, only the routing differs), and a live LLM injects variance you can't attribute, so "routing improved health" stops being provable. It also multiplies cost, QA, and iteration latency — all upstream of what's graded. LLM brains earn their cost only where emergence is the product: the one collusion case.
?"A rules engine isn't real AI — this isn't ambitious enough."▶
Your answer
It's plenty ambitious where it counts: tool-grounded LLM agents (the agentic + tool-use frontier) that emergently collude, plus the AI Investigator doing judgment-over-structure. We just don't spend agents on filler hands that only need to hit a VPIP target — a deterministic policy does that instantly. Restraint is the sophistication for a responsible-AI integrity product: the story is "we used AI exactly where it adds value, and kept it out of the detector on purpose."
?"An LLM bot would make a great training opponent for new players."▶
Your answer
Agreed that we want a great opponent — so the decisions should come from the engine + a solver baseline (Approach B), which actually plays well and does it deterministically. The LLM sits on top for coaching and table-talk. Decision-making and explanation are different jobs; we put each where it's strongest. An LLM making the play decisions would be weaker, slower, and unrepeatable.
?"How would we even know the generated hands are realistic?"▶
Your answer
This is the best question — and only the engine path can answer it. We fit per-stake VPIP/PFR/aggression distributions from the 21.6M real PHH hands, then check our generated hands fall in-distribution. That's a measurable, defensible realism test. With an LLM black box you have no distribution to check against — you're trusting vibes. Determinism plus a real reference corpus is what makes realism provable instead of asserted.
?"Isn't this just avoiding the hard AI work?"▶
Your answer
The opposite — we put the hard AI work exactly where it pays off: tool-grounded agents that emergently collude and the derivation of integrity signals from their real play, plus the AI Investigator explaining it without overclaiming. What we don't do is burn the budget making an LLM hallucinate legal betting sequences for 200 filler players — that's effort upstream of what's graded, for no gain.
